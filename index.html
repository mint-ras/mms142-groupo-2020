Augmented Reality

World of Extended Reality

Augmented reality is when parts of the users’ physical world is enhanced or extended with the use of computer-generated input. These inputs can be sound, video, graphics, GPS overlays and much more. This digital content then responds in real time to changes in the user’s environment, such as movement. The term “augmented reality” was first used to describe such technology in 1990 by Thomas Caudell.

Augmented Reality (AR) – Digital elements appear over real-world views, sometimes with limited interaction between them (often via smartphones).
Virtual Reality (VR) – An immersive experiences that isolates users from the real world (typically using headset devices).
Mixed Reality (MR) – A combination of AR & VR elements so digital objects can interact with the real world.

There is a slight overlap regarding interactivity. But in AR, digital elements are merely super-imposed on real world views, not anchored directly to them.


Augmented Reality as Disruptive Innovation

Augmented reality can be considered a disruptive innovation because AR can add value, solve problems & enhance the user experience in nearly every industry. Businesses are catching on & increasing investments to drive the growth of AR. As AR technology matures, the number of applications continues to grow and can influence our shopping, entertainment, work, & more.

Examples of AR in applications

Retail
AR allows users to try on virtually anything in retail from clothes to shoes to glasses to seeing how furniture would look in your home. Some retailers employing AR include Converse, Vyking, Nike, Watchbox, Speqs Eyewear, Warby Parker, IKEA, just to name a few. 72% of customers purchased products they hadn’t planned on after using AR while shopping, according to the study “The Impact of AR on Retail.”

Construction and training
In construction, AR allows architects, clients, crews, and developers to visualize a design in a space & existing conditions before any construction begins. AR & MR also provide valuable training tools for factory workers & technicians to complete tasks, troubleshoot problems, & digital references.

Tourism
AR can provide a potential traveler with an even more immersive experience from a destination before they travel.

Education
AR can help engage students with dynamic 3D models & benefit visual learners with digital renderings & life-like visualizations.

Healthcare
AR can provide surgeons with digital images & critical information within their field of view during a surgical procedure. Startups are building AR technology to support digital surgery, 3D medical imaging & specific surgeries.

Transportation/Navigation Systems
AR makes it possible for cars to project the outside camera view onto the interior car seats giving the illusion of a transparent car.

AR is impacting major industries, from retail to entertainment, such as PokemonGO and Snapchat filters. Bank of America Merrill Lynch expects AR to be an $8 billion “revenue opportunity” for Apple. Apple’s ARKit 2.0 is Apple’s developer platform for AR apps, thus stepping up its commitment to the technology. The partnership between developers & the large tech companies of Apple & Facebook has allowed AR applications to dream up, build, & create opportunities for games, play, business solutions & revenue opportunities.

In modern Filipino language, the Abakada alphabet is considered the modern Baybayin, as it is adopted to be used alongside the Filipino national language, Tagalog, in 1914. However, an actual modernized version of the Baybayin script exists.


The modernized version of Baybayin tends to differ to that of the the traditional way in a way that the latter's writing had a few modifications compared to the latter. The timeline of modern baybayin can be split into three parts, all which are subsequent revisions of the former revision based on Old baybayin: Semi-modernized Baybayin, Post-Modern Baybayin, and Advanced Modern Baybayin.


Semi Baybayin: Basing from the traditional way of writing Baybayin, the Semi-modern version starts to incorporate the Virama Kudlit (cross marks) alongside. Said marks were introduced by Francisco Lopez in 1620 as a way to cancel out pre-existent vowel sounds from the traditional.


Post-Modern Baybayin: The post-modern revision is then based on the semi-modernized version of the Script, to which it then added the use of modified characters alongside standardized ones.


Advance-Modern Baybayin: The final revision of baybayin, to which is considered as 'true' modernized Baybayin. Said revision is then based on 32 characters, including the characters of the abakada alphabet plus a few more characters derived from modern English alphabet (Lla, Cha, Tsa, Ca, Qa, and Xa).
